# Taiwan Forest Ecosystems

> **30-second overview:** Within a 200-kilometer horizontal distance, Taiwan rises from sea level to 3,952 meters, creating a complete forest spectrum from tropical to sub-arctic zones. With 60.71% forest coverage—double the global average—this island condenses almost all of Earth's forest types. From salt-resistant coastal Casuarina to thousand-year hinoki cypresses in the cloud belt, to Yushan junipers at 3,600 meters, this represents the richest ecological journey in the shortest distance on Earth.

## A Counter-Intuitive Truth

**Taiwan is one of the world's most forest-dense islands, yet also home to some of Earth's most vulnerable forests.**

2.197 million hectares of forest, 60.71% coverage, 502 million cubic meters of standing volume—these numbers place Taiwan 33rd globally in forest rankings, far exceeding the world average of 30.3%. Yet these forests are almost entirely squeezed onto vertical mountainsides, enduring annual tests of typhoons, earthquakes, and torrential rains, plus long-term threats from climate change.

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Many don't realize that Taiwan's forest coverage exceeds that of Germany (32%), France (31%), and even Norway (38%), famous for its forests. But Taiwan's forests grow almost entirely on slopes steeper than 30 degrees—such "vertical forests" are rare worldwide.

## From Coast to Sky: A 200-Kilometer Journey Through Time

Standing at Keelung Port, looking south, Yushan Main Peak lies 200 kilometers away. This distance equals Taipei to Hsinchu, but the elevation rises from 0 to 3,952 meters. If you could instantly traverse this vertical sequence, you'd experience complete climate zones from subtropical to sub-arctic.

### First Stop: Pioneer Corps in Salt Spray (0-100m)

The journey begins where waves crash against shores. This looks barren—sand, salt spray, strong winds—but look closer, and you'll discover a small pioneer army.

Casuarina needle-leaves sway in sea breezes. These aren't true leaves but degenerated branch tips that reduce salt damage. Pandanus trees grip loose sand with aerial roots, while Pittosporum's waxy leaves reflect sunlight. These plants lack magnificent stature but serve as the first defense line protecting the inland.

On the Hengchun Peninsula, you can still find Taiwan's last tropical forest remnants—Diospyros ferrea, Palaquium formosanum, Diospyros philippensis. These are Ice Age survivors; when the entire Northern Hemisphere froze, this island's southern tip became the final refuge for tropical plants.

### Second Stop: The Camphor Kingdom's Fragrant Realm (100-500m)

Leaving the coast and entering low-elevation hills, the air fills with a spicy fragrance. This is camphor's scent—the smell that once made Taiwan world-famous.

From the late 19th to early 20th century, Taiwan camphor comprised 70% of global production. After the Japanese arrived, they expanded harvesting of this "green gold." Camphor wasn't just fragrance but a key ingredient for celluloid (early plastic) and smokeless gunpowder. A thousand-year camphor tree could yield dozens of kilograms of camphor, enough to enrich a family.

Today, original camphor-nanmu forests are scarce. Most are secondary forests—regrowth after Japanese-era logging. But that fragrance lingers. Lagerstroemia subcostata's smooth bark is so slippery even monkeys can't climb it, while Bougainvillea's red flowers dot the forest, and tree fern fronds unfurl like umbrellas, reminding us these forests once resembled dinosaur-era landscapes.

### Third Stop: The Acorn Forest's Abundant Table (500-1,800m)

Climbing higher as temperatures drop, the forest's protagonists become Fagaceae plants. Cyclobalanopsis glauca, Quercus morii, Castanopsis carlesii—names unfamiliar to most, but their fruits you've surely seen: acorns.

This is Taiwan's wildlife restaurant. Each autumn, acorns rain down, attracting Formosan black bears, Formosan macaques, and wild boars for winter fat storage. Oak forest biodiversity is extraordinarily rich; a century-old oak can support hundreds of insect, bird, and small mammal species.

At this elevation, you encounter true giants. Quercus morii can reach 30 meters tall with trunk diameters exceeding 2 meters. Living up to 500 years, they've witnessed indigenous migrations, Han Chinese settlement, Japanese rule, and modern Taiwan's birth.

### Fourth Stop: The Rulers' Realm in the Clouds (1,800-2,500m)

Then you enter Taiwan's forest sanctuary—the cloud belt.

This is hinoki cypress territory. Taiwan red cypress (Chamaecyparis formosensis) and Taiwan hinoki (Chamaecyparis obtusa var. formosana)—Earth's only two Taiwan-endemic cypresses—found perfect survival conditions at this elevation. Annual rainfall of 3,000-5,000mm, relative humidity constantly above 80%, and clouds that rarely disperse.

**Why hinoki? Why here?**

The answer lies in water molecules. Clouds bring not just moisture but nutrients. Dust, pollen, and trace elements floating in fog are directly absorbed by leaves—this "fog fertilization" allows hinoki to grow into giants on barren ridges.

A 2,000-year Taiwan red cypress can reach 12 meters in diameter. Standing before it, you'd need 20 people holding hands to encircle it. Its lifespan spans all human civilization—when it sprouted, Jesus hadn't been born; when Han Chinese first arrived in Taiwan, it had already lived 1,600 years.

> **💡 Did You Know**
> Why do hinoki cypresses live so long? The secret lies in "hinokitiol." This natural chemical compound has powerful antibacterial and insect-repelling properties, preventing hinoki from rotting even after death. At Smangus, a thousand-year red cypress fell 300 years ago but remains wood-hard.

At Qilan Mountain, you can see Taiwan's most complete primary hinoki forest. These 15,000 hectares of never-logged forest were recommended as a World Heritage potential site by the Council for Cultural Affairs in 2003. Walking through, you understand what "sacred tree" means—not from superstition, but from awe. These trees survived ice ages, volcanic eruptions, and major earthquakes; they're living fossils of Earth's history.

But hinoki's story is also Taiwan's forests' most painful memory.

### Japan's 33-Year Harvest: Ecological Catastrophe Behind Numbers

In 1912, Alishan Forest began operations. Over 33 years, Japanese loggers harvested 9,773 hectares of Alishan forest, removing 3.47 million cubic meters of hinoki. To transport these giants, they built the Alishan Forest Railway—zigzag mountain climbing, one-eyed locomotives, wooden stations—today's tourist attractions originally served hinoki extraction.

Taipingshan Forest, Baxianshan Forest, plus Alishan formed "Taiwan's Three Great Forests." Conservative estimates suggest Japanese-era logging removed 6 million cubic meters of hinoki—equivalent to 3 million thousand-year giants. This timber built Japanese shrines and palaces, even crossing oceans to support Japanese military expansion.

**When a thousand-year giant falls, its crash echoes 5 kilometers away.**

Loggers of that era said whenever a great hinoki fell, the entire valley thundered, wildlife scattered in panic, as if mountain spirits were weeping. Indigenous peoples were especially heartbroken—their generations-worshipped sacred trees became lumber, shipped downhill to build invaders' houses.

Post-war, the ROC government continued forest operations until 1991's official "complete natural forest logging ban," ending nearly a century of large-scale harvesting. But Taiwan had already lost over 95% of its primary hinoki forests.

### Fifth Stop: Coniferous Forest's Ultimate Challenge (2,500-3,600m)

Leaving hinoki territory and climbing higher, you enter Taiwan's frigid zone—home to hemlock and fir.

Taiwan hemlock resembles Christmas trees but endures harsher conditions than Nordic spruces. Above 2,500 meters elevation, winter temperatures drop to -10°C, with constant strong winds and intense UV radiation. Trees must complete annual growth in brief summers while conserving energy through long winters.

Taiwan fir dominates this forest belt, forming vast pure stands that look like dark green seas from afar. Each tree maintains perfect conical shape—not for beauty but to shed snow efficiently, preventing branch breakage.

On Snow Mountain, Hehuanshan, and Yushan, you witness Taiwan fir forest spectacles. Particularly after morning fog clears and sunlight strikes these dark green spires, the entire forest gleams metallic. This is Taiwan's closest approximation to Nordic forests, yet located on a subtropical island at 23°N.

### Final Stop: Sky City's Last Guardians (Above 3,600m)

Above 3,600 meters, true forest ends, but final guardians remain—Yushan junipers.

These trees no longer pursue towering height but creep along the ground, forming green carpets. Gales can't topple them because they grow lower than wind; frost can't kill them because their cell sap contains natural antifreeze; scorching sun can't desiccate them because waxy needles reflect harsh light.

Standing near Yushan Main Peak, overlooking Yushan juniper groves below, you discover something remarkable: these seemingly dwarf shrubs may be older than giant trees downslope. A Yushan juniper appearing only half a meter tall might have lived 500 years. They're forest frontier sentinels, marking woody plant survival limits.

## Taiwan Cedar: Earth's Most Ancient Tree's Final Sanctuary

In this vertical journey, one species deserves special mention: Taiwan cedar.

Taiwan cedar is among Earth's oldest tree species, once widely distributed across the Northern Hemisphere. During the Cretaceous period, dinosaurs roamed Taiwan cedar forests. But when ice ages arrived, Northern Hemisphere Taiwan cedars nearly vanished entirely, surviving only in refuges like Taiwan and southwestern China.

Today, Taiwan's Daan River watershed harbors a small Taiwan cedar forest, including the "Taiwan Cedar Three Sisters" reaching 80 meters—Taiwan's tallest giants. These living fossils witnessed 65 million years of Earth's changes, predating human civilization and Himalayan uplift.

> **⚠️ Controversial Viewpoint**
> Taiwan cedar conservation opinions differ. Conservationists advocate strict protection with zero human interference; some scholars favor moderate scientific research and educational access to raise species awareness. Balancing conservation with education remains a long-term challenge for Taiwan's forests.

## Cloud Forest Climate Change Crisis

Taiwan forests face their greatest threat not from logging but climate change.

According to National Taiwan University Forestry Department research, Taiwan temperatures will rise 2-4°C over the next 50 years, causing massive upward shifts in forest distribution. Latest computer simulations show:

- **Spruce forests will shrink 77-82%**, nearly facing extinction
- **Hinoki forests will decline 52-54%**, with drastically reduced ranges
- **Low-elevation broadleaf forests will expand 37%**, invading upward

> **📊 Data Source**
> These predictions come from Professor Chia-Chun Hsu's research team at NTU Forestry Department, published in The Reporter's 2022 special report. Research used IPCC climate models and Taiwan's 30-year forest survey data.

**Most worrying is high-mountain plants' future.** Already living on peaks with nowhere higher to migrate, Yushan junipers and Taiwan firs may face "nowhere to retreat" by century's end.

## Modern Forest Threats: The Shadow of Illegal Logging

Even under complete logging bans, Taiwan forests face illegal harvesting threats.

In May 2022, police arrested two escaped migrant workers in Alishan, who had sawed 30 kilograms of hinoki worth approximately NT$1.04 million in rainy darkness. In 2025, Miaoli saw Taiwan's largest recent illegal logging case—a mountain bandit group harvesting over 60,000 kilograms of precious timber.

**Why do people still risk illegal logging?**

The answer lies in enormous profit differentials. A hinoki burl's "mountain price" might be tens of thousands of NT dollars, but after carving, market prices can soar to millions. Despite 2015 Forest Act amendments imposing up to 10 years 6 months imprisonment and NT$20 million fines for precious species theft, windfall profits still tempt risk-takers.

More heartbreaking, this illegal harvesting targets the rarest species. Taiwan red cypress faces massive poaching as host to valuable Antrodia cinnamomea fungus, now extremely rare in wild habitats. Some thousand-year red cypresses are "skinned" for burls by poachers—trees survive but bear permanent scars.

## Indigenous Forest Wisdom

Discussing Taiwan forests requires acknowledging indigenous contributions.

Atayal tribes have gaga (communal norms) protecting hinoki sacred trees, Tsou tribes designate no-cut zones maintaining ecological balance, and Paiwan tribes developed exquisite wood carving cultures. This traditional ecological knowledge often understands forest mysteries more profoundly than modern science.

**Smangus' hinoki conservation exemplifies this perfectly.**

After discovering giant tree groves, this Atayal tribe chose not to log and sell but developed ecotourism, making giants their economic pillars. Today, Smangus is called "God's Tribe," attracting tens of thousands of annual pilgrims to thousand-year hinoki cypresses.

Indigenous forest perspectives differ importantly from modern conservation: they don't equate "protection" with "hands-off." Moderate harvesting, rotation cutting, and forest management all traditionally maintain forest health. This "living with forests" wisdom is being rediscovered by modern conservation science.

## Forest Futures: Challenges and Hope

Taiwan's forest story tells of loss and renewal.

We lost 95% of primary hinoki forests, but the remaining 5% enjoys strict protection. We can't reverse climate change but can build ecological corridors helping species migration. We can't stop all illegal logging but can educate more people about forest values.

Today's Taiwan forests write new chapters:

**Restoration programs proceed.** The Forestry and Nature Conservation Agency promotes "forest restoration," planting native species in suitable locations. New Taipei's Fushan Botanical Garden and Nantou's Lienhuachi represent successful forest restoration examples.

**Technology assists protection.** Satellite monitoring, drone patrols, and AI recognition systems help rangers monitor forest conditions more effectively. Fire or illegal logging triggers alerts within minutes.

**Younger generations awaken.** More Taiwanese youth dedicate themselves to forest conservation, not just as passive protectors but active innovators. Using VR to take people "into" primary forests, podcasts telling forest stories, social media promoting ecotourism—new generations protect forests their way.

## Journey's End and Beginning

From sea-level Casuarina to Yushan-peak junipers, we've completed this 200-kilometer vertical journey. In 15 minutes of reading, you've experienced Earth's most condensed ecological diversity.

Taiwan's forest story teaches us: **nature's richness and vulnerability often coexist.** 60.71% forest coverage makes this island verdant, but climate change threats cloud future certainty. Thousand-year hinoki majesty inspires awe, yet they nearly vanished to chainsaw sounds.

Every tree is a history book recording Earth's changes, human footprints, and life's tenacity. Next time you wander Taiwan's forests, remember you're walking on Earth's most spectacular natural stage. This isn't just trees' home but evolution's museum, climate change's frontline, and humanity-nature relationship's testing ground.

Protecting Taiwan's forests means safeguarding a microcosm of Earth's biodiversity. On this 36,000-square-kilometer island lies condensed imagination and responsibility for our planet's future.
