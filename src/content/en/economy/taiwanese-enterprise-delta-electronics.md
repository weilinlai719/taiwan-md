On April 4, 1971, in a 15-ping sheet metal factory in Xinzhuang, New Taipei City, 35-year-old Bruce Cheng and two partners were assembling television transformers. This man, who had fled to Taiwan as a 13-year-old war refugee and worked his way through university, had just NT$300,000 in startup capital. He never imagined that 55 years later, this small workshop would become the world's largest switching power supply manufacturer, and his name would be etched on an asteroid, flying through space forever.

**Delta Electronics' story demonstrates how Taiwan enterprises can redefine manufacturing value—evolving from OEM thinking to innovation breakthrough, from survival to sustainability.**

## The Invisible Power Empire

When you charge your phone, you might be using Delta's technology. When Google's AI processes your search queries, the data center power systems behind it likely come from Delta. From personal computers to supercomputer data centers, from Tesla charging stations to wind power converters, Delta Electronics has quietly become the "invisible infrastructure" of modern digital life.

This Taipei Neihu-headquartered company achieved NT$370.8 billion (approximately $12 billion USD) in revenue in 2023, employs about 88,000 people globally, and operates over 200 facilities in 38 countries. In the global switching power supply market, Delta holds nearly 25% market share, maintaining its position as world leader. Google, Microsoft, and Amazon data centers are all its customers.

**But what's more remarkable than these numbers is the company's transformation trajectory.**

## From TV Components to AI Era's Power Butler

When Delta was founded in 1971, it primarily produced TV coils and intermediate frequency transformers—components for black-and-white television sets. But in 1975, Bruce Cheng made a company-destiny-changing decision: transitioning to switching power supply technology.

This decision appeared to be a technical detail but was actually a business revolution. Traditional linear power supplies were bulky and inefficient; switching power supplies were compact and highly efficient. When personal computers emerged in the 1980s, Delta was perfectly positioned at the wind's mouth, becoming a power supplier for Apple, Compaq, and other brands.

**The real strategic turning point occurred in 2000.** While the dot-com bubble had just burst and many tech companies were contracting, Bruce Cheng proposed a radical business mission: "To provide innovative, clean and energy-efficient solutions for a better tomorrow." In an era when ESG concepts weren't yet widespread, this decision was viewed by many as "too ahead of its time."

Looking back 20 years later, this was Delta's wisest strategy. When the world began focusing on climate change and energy efficiency, Delta already had 20 years of technological accumulation and brand positioning. It evolved from a simple component supplier to a leader in energy management solutions.

## The Entrepreneur on an Asteroid

On April 1, 2006, Taiwan's Central University Lulin Observatory discovered an asteroid, numbered 168126. After certification by the International Astronomical Union, this asteroid was named "Chengbruce" after Bruce Cheng.

This wasn't vanity purchased with money, but specific recognition for his decade-plus support of astronomical research. He funded the construction of Lulin Observatory's 2-meter telescope, rebuilt Central University's Science Building 1 observatory, and established the "Delta Young Astronomer Lectureship Award." During the UN's 2009 "International Year of Astronomy," he even served as Taiwan's "Starlight Ambassador."

**This reveals Bruce Cheng's thinking framework that transcends business.** He believes in the importance of basic scientific research for humanity's future, even when short-term commercial returns aren't visible. This long-term thinking also explains why Delta can maintain its technological leadership.

## Breakthrough Through Contradiction: OEM Giant's Brand Journey

Delta's greatest challenge was finding balance between OEM thinking and brand innovation. From the 1980s-2000s, it was primarily an ODM supplier for multinational corporations, bringing stable orders but also limiting technological development autonomy.

**The turning point came in the 21st century.** Delta began heavily investing in its own "DELTA" brand while maintaining OEM business. This dual-track strategy allowed it to maintain cash flow while accumulating brand value. In emerging fields like industrial automation, EV charging, and renewable energy, Delta directly entered international markets with its own brand.

Currently, Delta holds about 20% market share in Europe's EV charging market and ranks among the top three globally in UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) markets. These achievements prove that Taiwan enterprises aren't limited to OEM work—they can also establish brand advantages in technology-intensive fields.

## The Sustainability Revolution Behind the Numbers

Delta's most stunning figure isn't revenue, but its contribution to global energy conservation. According to official statistics, from 2010 to 2023, Delta's high-efficiency products cumulatively saved customers over 40 billion kWh globally, equivalent to reducing 21 million tons of carbon emissions—exceeding many countries' annual total emissions.

In 2021, Delta became Taiwan's first manufacturing company to join the RE100 initiative, committing to 100% renewable energy use across global operations by 2030. It's among the few companies simultaneously possessing renewable energy technical capabilities and signing Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs).

**More importantly, Delta transformed sustainability from slogan to business model.** Its power efficiency technology, data center cooling systems, and EV charging solutions are all products born from climate change response needs. Environmental protection isn't a cost—it's a source of competitive advantage.

## New Opportunities in the AI Era

With artificial intelligence applications exploding, global data center power demand is growing dramatically. Tech giants like Meta and Google are building ultra-large data centers with 1GW power consumption—equivalent to a city of 260,000 people.

Delta has benefited significantly from this AI wave. In Q3 2024, its data center-related product revenue grew over 30%. Delta's high-efficiency power supplies and liquid cooling technologies have become key components for AI servers.

**But Delta's challenge is maintaining leadership amid rapid technological change.** New-generation AI chips demand higher power management requirements, jumping from traditional 400-800 watts to 8,000 watts or higher. Delta must continuously innovate to maintain advantages in this fast-growing market.

## Succession and Legacy

In 2012, Bruce Cheng announced retirement, transferring management to his eldest son, Hai-Ying Cheng (鄭平). This represents a rare smooth succession case in Taiwan's business world. Hai-Ying worked at Delta for over 20 years, starting from grassroots engineer positions, developing deep understanding of company culture and technology.

Under Hai-Ying's leadership, Delta continues deepening its "environmental protection, energy conservation, love the earth" mission while strengthening digital transformation and AI applications. In December 2024, Delta launched its "Delta Net Zero Science Laboratory" in Tainan Science Park—Taiwan's first MW-class water electrolysis hydrogen production and fuel cell testing platform.

**This shows Delta isn't just executing existing strategies but preparing for the next generation's energy revolution.**

## Victory of Systems Thinking

Delta's success secret lies in "systems integration" capabilities. It's not just a component supplier but a systems company capable of providing complete solutions. From power modules to complete energy management systems, from hardware to software, Delta has built a comprehensive technical platform.

This systems thinking enables it to address customers' increasingly complex needs. When automotive manufacturers need EV powertrain systems, Delta can provide complete solutions from onboard chargers to drive motors. When data centers need energy-saving renovations, Delta can offer one-stop services from power supplies to air conditioning.

**In the globally specialized industrial chain, systems integration capability has become a scarce resource.**

## Future Challenges

While Delta enjoys advantages in both technology and markets, challenges cannot be underestimated. Geopolitical risks, raw material shortages, and talent competition all test this globalized enterprise's resilience.

More importantly, as AI, IoT, and EV applications rapidly develop, power technology evolution is also accelerating. From silicon-based semiconductors to silicon carbide (SiC) and gallium nitride (GaN), from traditional control to AI optimization, Delta must continuously invest in R&D to maintain technological leadership.

**But if you understand this company's history, you'll be confident in its adaptability.** From TV components to AI era's power butler, from NT$300,000 to hundreds of billions in revenue, Delta has proven it can find opportunities amid change.

## Epitome of an Era

Delta Electronics' story epitomizes Taiwan manufacturing's transformation and upgrading. It demonstrates how Taiwan enterprises evolved from OEM thinking to technological innovation, from cost competition to value creation, from local operations to global deployment.

More importantly, Delta proved that "manufacturing" and "sustainable development" aren't opposing forces but can mutually reinforce each other. In a global era pursuing net-zero emissions, Delta's energy-saving technology isn't just products—it's humanity's tool for addressing climate change.

**A war orphan who fled at age 13 used half a century to build a power empire and put his name in space.** This story tells us Taiwan isn't just a "manufacturing island" but an "innovation island." In the next generation's technological revolution, we have reason to expect more such Taiwan legends.
