# Taiwan National Scenic Areas System

> **30-Second Overview:** Taiwan has 13 "National Scenic Areas" established since 1984,
> covering over 140,000 hectares and serving millions of visitors annually.
> Unlike "National Parks," these scenic areas prioritize tourism over conservation—
> a seemingly contradictory positioning that represents Taiwan's greatest
> management challenge after 40 years of tourism development.

What do you expect from a place with "National" in its name? World-class protection standards? Strict environmental controls?

Taiwan's National Scenic Areas might surprise you with their answer: **They weren't created to protect—they were created to make money.**

In 1984, Taiwan's first National Scenic Area was born at the Northeast Coast. This was just before martial law ended, as Taiwan's economy was booming and the government realized tourism could be the next golden industry. Thus began a scenic area system designed primarily for "tourism development."

40 years later, these 13 scenic areas have become pillars of Taiwan's tourism industry, but they also face unprecedented challenges: How to balance profit with protection?

## A Contradictory Beginning

In 1973, Taiwan enacted the Tourism Development Act, establishing the legal foundation for scenic areas. The thinking was straightforward: **Taiwan has beautiful scenery—why not develop it for tourism?**

Eleven years later, the Northeast Coast National Scenic Area Administration was established, marking the beginning of Taiwan's systematic scenic area development. Choosing the Northeast Coast wasn't coincidental—it was only an hour from Taipei, featured unique coastal erosion landscapes, and had the cultural history of Jiufen and Jinguashi, making it an ideal testing ground for tourism development.

> **💡 Did You Know?**
> The key difference between Taiwan's National Scenic Areas and National Parks lies in their
> management goals: National Parks are managed by the Ministry of Interior's Construction
> and Planning Agency, prioritizing ecological conservation; National Scenic Areas are
> managed by the Tourism Administration under the Ministry of Transportation, prioritizing
> tourism development. Same "National" label, vastly different protection standards.

But this system design embedded contradictions from the start.

The Taiwan Environmental Information Center's criticism hit the nail on the head: "Current National Scenic Areas like Alishan and Sun Moon Lake merely added the National Scenic Area designation to their names and built beautiful visitor centers, but lack matching comprehensive tourism information, in-depth activities, and complete interpretation services."

In other words, **Taiwan gave these places a "National" title without providing corresponding management standards.**

## The Era of Rapid Expansion

In the 1990s, Taiwan's economy boomed, per capita income rose dramatically, and leisure travel demand surged. The government capitalized on this momentum to promote scenic area development:

- **1991**: North Coast and Guanyinshan National Scenic Area
- **1995**: Penghu National Scenic Area
- **1996**: Tri-Mountain National Scenic Area
- **1997**: Dapeng Bay and East Longitudinal Valley National Scenic Areas
- **1999**: Alishan and Matsu National Scenic Areas

Each scenic area establishment represented government emphasis on the tourism industry. But it also meant more previously pristine natural environments were brought under "development and utilization."

This process wasn't without controversy. The Tri-Mountain National Scenic Area, for instance, encompasses three completely different areas: Lion's Head Mountain, Bagua Mountain, and Lishan. Lishan, at over 2,000 meters elevation, was originally a high-altitude agricultural area. After becoming part of the scenic area, temperate fruit industries flourished, but also brought soil and water conservation concerns.

**The key contradiction: Scenic areas must "protect" scenery while simultaneously "developing" it.** These two goals are inherently conflicting.

## The Present State of 13 Scenic Areas

Today's Taiwan National Scenic Areas system includes 13 administrative offices, spanning from sea level coasts to mountains above 2,000 meters, from volcanic geology to coral reef ecosystems, covering almost every landscape type in Taiwan.

According to the Tourism Administration's 2023 report, these scenic areas gradually recovered post-pandemic:

**Key Statistics (2023):**
- Northeast and Yilan Coast: Taiwan Tourist Shuttle served 158,007 passengers
- Sun Moon Lake: Served over 78,000 visitors, cable car carried 769,323 passengers
- Alishan: Attracted 5.47 million visitors, generated approximately NT$11.8 billion in tourism revenue
- Penghu: Various events attracted over 100,000 visitors, generating NT$520 million in economic value

These numbers look impressive, but the underlying pressure is very real.

> **⚠️ Controversial Viewpoint**
> In 2023, Penghu National Scenic Area cleaned 1,650.9 tons of marine debris from
> 1,894 kilometers of coastline. While this shows management efforts, it also
> reveals the actual environmental impact of tourism pressure.

## The Unspoken Costs

Taiwan National Scenic Areas' success is often measured by "visitor numbers" and "tourism revenue." But some costs cannot be captured in statistics.

**The Qingjing phenomenon** is the most obvious example. In Qingjing, Ren'ai Township, over 100 European-style homestays operate within 4 kilometers, with only 34 being legal. Most are built on 50-60% steep slopes, far exceeding the 30% legal limit.

More seriously, Qingjing has no sewage treatment system—all wastewater is discharged directly into the Zhuoshui River. Intensive development has expanded impermeable surfaces, causing surface runoff to carry massive amounts of sediment, severely silting the downstream Wanda Reservoir.

Director Chi Po-lin's documented scenes in "Beyond Beauty" were shocking: right next to Alishan's Zhushan Station was a large area of dangerous slope collapse.

**This is the fundamental dilemma facing Taiwan's National Scenic Areas system: Tourism brings revenue, but also environmental costs. And these costs are often borne by the next generation.**

> **📝 Curator's Note**
> Wu Nien-jen's narration in "Beyond Beauty" remains powerful today: "When we treat
> staying in mountain homestays, drinking high-altitude tea, and eating mountain
> vegetables as fashionable leisure activities, we're all accomplices in destroying
> this land." This harsh truth reveals how consumer choices determine environmental futures.

## Transformation Efforts

Facing environmental pressure challenges, the National Scenic Areas system hasn't remained passive. Recently, "sustainable development," "ecotourism," and "green certification" have become development priorities across scenic areas.

**Specific actions include:**

- **Northeast Coast**: Achieved Green Destinations Gold Award certification (2024-2026), promoted 14 businesses to obtain Green Travel Seals
- **Southwest Coast**: Achieved Bronze Green Travel Destination certification, "Protecting Stone Weirs" won Global Top 100 Sustainable Tourism Story Award  
- **Sun Moon Lake**: Achieved Silver Award for Global Top 100 Green Tourism Destinations, Environmental Education Center passed excellent evaluation

But the real challenge isn't technical—it's conceptual.

**How to help visitors understand the value of "slow"?** How to get businesses to accept "less" profit? How to get government to acknowledge that "economic growth" isn't the only indicator of tourism development success?

The answers to these questions will determine the future of Taiwan's National Scenic Areas system.

## New Possibilities in the Digital Age

In 2023, all scenic areas promoted "smart tourism." Northeast Coast established real-time crowd flow analysis systems, North Coast provided real-time video and parking information, and Sun Moon Lake launched PWA mobile travel services.

These technology applications aren't just for visitor convenience—more importantly, they enable **carrying capacity management**. Through real-time data, management units can provide early warnings when visitor numbers exceed capacity, guide crowd dispersal, and reduce environmental pressure.

But technology is just a tool—the key is transforming management thinking.

> **💡 Did You Know?**
> In 2023, Taiwan's National Scenic Areas certified 177 "excellent public restrooms."
> This seemingly trivial number actually reflects infrastructure quality improvement.
> Good restrooms aren't just part of visitor experience—they're concrete practices
> of environmental hygiene and sustainable management.

## Reflection After 40 Years

From 1984 to 2024, Taiwan's National Scenic Areas system has completed 40 years. It successfully made Taiwan's beautiful scenery visible to more people, created considerable economic benefits, and nurtured an entire generation of tourism professionals.

But 40 years of development also exposed systemic problems: **fundamental contradictions between tourism and conservation, coordination difficulties between central and local governments, conflicts between short-term benefits and long-term sustainability.**

The Taiwan Environmental Information Center's 2004 criticism remains relevant today: "National Scenic Areas are gateways for people's tourism and leisure. If these national-level scenic areas can integrate information, implement interpretation work, and thoroughly reduce environmental impact, they would provide an excellent demonstration and beginning for promoting in-depth tourism."

Twenty years later, how much have we achieved?

## The Next 40 Years

Climate change, pandemic impacts, and international competition make the challenges facing Taiwan's National Scenic Areas system more complex than ever. But opportunities are also greater.

**New generation visitors value sustainability more, are willing to pay for quality, and prefer in-depth experiences over superficial tourism.** This provides opportunities for Taiwan's National Scenic Areas transformation.

The key question: Can we shift from "quantity" growth to "quality" improvement? From "development" thinking to "management" thinking? From "short-term profits" to "long-term value"?

The future of Taiwan's National Scenic Areas system isn't just about 13 scenic areas—it's a microcosm of how all of Taiwan faces the eternal contradiction between development and protection.

**On this beautiful island, each of us is both part of the scenery and a guardian of the scenery.**
